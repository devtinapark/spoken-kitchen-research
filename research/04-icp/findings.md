## The buyer is not the user

Consistent across every thread: the **buyer is the adult child (30s–50s)**; the **subject
is the aging parent/grandparent**. The transaction is emotional and time-pressured.

## Voice of customer (verbatim signal)

- *"My grandmother passed away last month at 96. While cleaning out her house, nobody
  [had her recipes]…"* — the **regret-after-loss** moment. Recurring, raw, frequent.
- *"AITAH if I keep my grandmother's recipe box?"* / *"I wish she had written down her
  recipes before…"* — the **recipe box as contested heirloom**.
- *"How do I keep these handwritten recipes from my grandma?"* — active, unsolved task.
- *"Translating recipes into English… they copy and paste into the translator"* — buyers
  **already DIY the bilingual step** with generic tools.
- *"Newer generations lose a sense of culture… not learning the language"* — the
  **heritage-loss anxiety** that makes translation a feature, not a nice-to-have.

## Primary persona — "The Bridge Daughter"

| | |
|---|---|
| **Who** | 2nd-gen immigrant, 30–50, US-based, adult child of an aging immigrant parent |
| **Trigger** | Parent aging/health scare; a holiday; a death in the family |
| **Job-to-be-done** | Capture Mom/Grandma's recipes *in her own voice and language* before they're lost, and make them *cookable by my English-speaking kids* |
| **Pain today** | Recipes are oral or handwritten in another language; manual transcription + DIY AI translation is tedious and lossy |
| **Willingness to pay** | High — already the StoryWorth/Remento gift buyer ($99–199), but underserved on language + recipes |
| **Emotional driver** | Guilt/urgency ("before it's too late") + identity preservation for the next generation |

### Secondary personas
- **The Heritage Parent** — wants their kids to keep the culture; buys for themselves.
- **The Diaspora Sibling Group** — pooling to gift a shared family cookbook.

## Where they congregate (channels, from the data)

| Channel | Evidence |
|---|---|
| **Reddit** | r/-style threads on preserving handwritten recipes, grief over lost recipes, AITAH recipe-box posts (`voc-recipes`) |
| **Facebook Groups** | "Preserving family recipes for future generations," recipe-preservation + contest groups (`community`) |
| **Instagram / YouTube** | Creators on cultural heritage cooking; "help preserve family recipes" posts (`voc-translate`) |
| **Heritage-language & 2nd-gen identity communities** | heritage-language forums, cultural-identity discussions (`voc-immigrant`) |
| **Gift-guide surfaces** | NYT/lifestyle "gifts for aging parents/grandparents" — hard-to-shop-for intent (`gifting`) |

→ Acquisition implication: **content + community**, not paid search against Mixbook. Meet
buyers in the grief/heritage conversations they're *already having*.

## Market size (sourced signal)

- **~20M adult second-generation Americans** (Pew, the foundational segment) and growing —
  immigrants were parents of **23% of all US children** (2009 Census via Wikipedia).
- Immigrants are a **>50% share of some metro populations** (TCF) — dense, targetable
  communities.
- This is the **bilingual-native sub-segment** of the proven StoryWorth/Remento gifting
  market — large enough to be a beachhead, specific enough to win uncontested.

## ICP takeaways feeding the thesis

1. The emotional trigger (aging elder + lost recipes) is **identical** to the proven
   legacy-gifting market — so demand and price tolerance are already validated.
2. The **bilingual/translation need is the wedge**: buyers do it manually because no
   product owns it.
3. Go-to-market is **community-led** (Reddit/FB/heritage groups), not SEM.

→ Phase 5 maps this ICP's unmet needs against the competitor capability gaps.
